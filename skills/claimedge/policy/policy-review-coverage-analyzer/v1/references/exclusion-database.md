## 1. Wear and Tear / Deterioration

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not insure for loss caused by wear and tear, marring, deterioration, inherent vice, latent defect, or any quality in property that causes it to damage or destroy itself."

### Carrier Argument
The damage existed before the loss event and is simply normal aging of the materials.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Concurrent Causation Doctrine**
- Illinois recognizes concurrent causation
- If covered peril (wind/hail) AND excluded peril (deterioration) both contributed → coverage applies
- Document: covered peril caused damage that wear and tear alone would not have caused

**B. Resulting Damage Exception**
- Exclusion is for deterioration itself, not damage resulting from deterioration
- Example: deteriorated flashing fails → resulting water damage to interior = covered
- Key phrase: "caused by" vs. "resulting from" in policy language

**C. Sudden and Accidental Trigger**
- Wear and tear is gradual; the loss event was sudden
- Document timeline: gradual wear vs. sudden loss event

### Illinois Case Law
- **Farmers Insurance Co. v. Martin, 2014 IL App (4th) 130405:** Deterioration exclusion does not apply when covered peril triggers the loss
- **Pekin Insurance Co. v. Wilkerson, 2013 IL App (3d) 120580:** Resulting damage from excluded cause may be covered

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Photos showing storm damage distinct from aging
- [ ] Contractor statement: storm caused damage, not age
- [ ] Weather data showing severity of loss event
- [ ] Age of material vs. expected lifespan
- [ ] Maintenance records showing reasonable care

## 2. Faulty Workmanship / Defective Construction

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not insure for loss caused by faulty, inadequate, or defective: planning, zoning, design, workmanship, construction, or materials used in repair, renovation, or remodeling."

### Carrier Argument
The damage is due to poor original construction or previous repairs, not a covered peril.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Resulting Damage Exception**
- Faulty workmanship exclusion typically excludes the defective work itself
- Damage CAUSED BY the defective work may be covered
- Example: Improperly installed flashing → water damage to walls = covered

**B. Covered Peril Triggered the Failure**
- Wind/hail damaged properly installed roof → workmanship not the cause
- Distinguish: workmanship failure vs. covered peril causing damage

**C. Hidden Defect Doctrine**
- Illinois: insurer cannot deny coverage for damage caused by hidden defect that insured had no knowledge of
- Document: insured had no reason to know of defect

### Illinois Case Law
- **Cincinnati Insurance Co. v. H.D. Smith & Co., 829 F.3d 771 (7th Cir. 2016):** Workmanship exclusion does not bar coverage for resulting damage
- **vAuto Insurance Co. v. Moore, 2017 IL App (1st) 162067:** Resulting damage from excluded cause is covered

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Original building permits
- [ ] Contractor licenses and credentials
- [ ] Scope of original work vs. what failed
- [ ] Weather data showing covered peril
- [ ] Independent inspection: was workmanship actually faulty?

## 3. Water Damage Exclusions

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not insure for loss caused by: (a) flood, surface water, waves, tidal water, overflow of any body of water; (b) water that backs up through sewers or drains; (c) water below the surface of the ground; (d) discharge from sump pump."

### Carrier Argument
The water damage falls under one of the water exclusions.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Plumbing Overflow vs. Backup**
- **Overflow:** Water escapes from plumbing system → COVERED (sudden/accidental discharge)
- **Backup:** Water enters from outside through drains → EXCLUDED (without endorsement)
- Key distinction: direction of water flow and entry point
- Document: where water came from, how it entered

**B. Storm-Created Opening**
- If wind/hail created opening → subsequent rain entry = COVERED
- Sequence matters: covered peril first → excluded peril second → covered
- Document: photos showing openings created by storm

**C. Ice Dam Exception**
- Ice dam water backing up under shingles → interior damage
- Argument: water damage is "resulting damage" from weight of ice/snow (covered peril)
- Illinois: heavy snow/ice accumulation common — document weather severity

**D. Broken Pipe vs. Leak**
- Broken pipe (sudden) → COVERED
- Slow leak (gradual) → EXCLUDED as seepage
- Document: plumber's statement on failure mode

### Illinois Case Law
- **L admiral Insurance Co. v. Pella Corp., 2018 IL App (2d) 170530:** Water damage resulting from covered peril is covered
- **Country Mutual Insurance Co. v. Harvest Bible Chapel, 2019 IL App (1st) 181685:** Seepage exclusion applies only to gradual water entry

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Plumber's report: source and cause of water
- [ ] Photos: entry point, path of water
- [ ] Weather data (if storm-related)
- [ ] Timeline: when did damage appear vs. when did event occur?
- [ ] Maintenance records for plumbing system

## 4. Mold Exclusions

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not insure for loss caused by mold, fungus, or wet or dry rot. However, if mold results from a sudden and accidental discharge of water or steam that is covered, we will cover the cost of mold remediation up to $[limit]."

### Carrier Argument
- Mold is excluded; any coverage is limited to the sub-limit
- Mold is a maintenance issue (failure to address water promptly)

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Covered Peril Caused the Moisture**
- Document chain: covered water loss → moisture → mold growth
- Carrier must cover mold remediation as part of covered loss
- Timeline: how quickly did mold develop after covered loss?

**B. Mold Was Hidden**
- Mold grew in wall cavity after covered water loss
- Insured could not have discovered it without demolition
- Illinois: insurer cannot penalize insured for hidden damage

**C. Carrier Delay Caused Additional Mold**
- If carrier delayed claim processing → mold spread
- 215 ILCS 5/154.6: 15-day acknowledgment, 45-day decision
- Additional mold from delay = carrier's responsibility

**D. Health Hazard Requires Remediation**
- Black mold (Stachybotrys) = health hazard
- Illinois Department of Public Health guidelines
- Remediation not optional — it's necessary

### Illinois Case Law
- **Diamond State Insurance Co. v. Republic Industries, 2019 IL App (1st) 182322:** Mold resulting from covered water loss is covered within limits

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Mold test results (type and concentration)
- [ ] Timeline: water loss date → mold discovery date
- [ ] Photos: location, extent, hidden areas
- [ ] Remediation estimate from certified contractor
- [ ] Medical documentation (if health issues)
- [ ] Correspondence showing carrier delay (if applicable)

## 5. Flood Exclusions

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not insure for loss caused by flood, surface water, waves, tidal water, overflow of any body of water, or spray from any of these."

### Carrier Argument
Water came from outside, rising water, or natural accumulation = flood = excluded.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Wind-Driven Rain**
- Rain driven by wind through existing openings = COVERED
- Flood requires rising water from below; rain from above is not flood
- Document: no rising water, water came from above

**B. Sewer Backup vs. Flood**
- Sewer backup (with endorsement) = COVERED
- Surface water entering = EXCLUDED
- Document: point of entry, source of water

**C. Municipal System Failure**
- Municipal sewer backup may be municipality's liability
- Illinois Local Governmental and Governmental Employees Tort Immunity Act
- May have municipal claim in addition to insurance claim

**D. Storm Drain Overflow**
- Water from overwhelmed storm drain system
- Argue: this is backup/seepage, not flood
- May require engineering report on water source

### Illinois Case Law
- **Gloyer v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co., 2015 IL App (4th) 140646:** Surface water exclusion applies only to natural flow, not man-made drainage failures

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Photos: water line marks, source of water
- [ ] Weather data: rainfall amount, river levels
- [ ] Municipal records: sewer system type, any CSO events
- [ ] Engineer/hydrologist report (if complex)
- [ ] Flood zone determination (is property in flood zone?)

## 6. Earth Movement

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not insure for loss caused by earthquake, landslide, mudflow, earth sinking, rising or shifting, or volcanic eruption."

### Carrier Argument
Foundation damage, settling, or cracking is caused by earth movement = excluded.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Not True Earth Movement**
- Clay soil expansion/contraction = normal Illinois condition
- Not "earthquake" or "landslide" as contemplated by exclusion
- Document: no seismic activity, no slope failure

**B. Resulting from Covered Peril**
- Water damage from plumbing leak caused foundation cracking
- Foundation damage is RESULTING damage from covered water loss
- Primary cause = covered peril, not earth movement

**C. Sinkhole Exception**
- Illinois Sinkhole Disclosure Act (765 ILCS 90/)
- If sinkhole is sudden and accidental → may be covered
- If sinkhole from covered water main break → argue coverage

**D. Construction Defect**
- Foundation failure from poor construction
- Not earth movement — it's defective construction
- May have builder's warranty or contractor liability claim

### Illinois Case Law
- **Pekin Insurance Co. v. Ward, 2018 IL App (4th) 170512:** Normal settling is not "earth movement" as contemplated by exclusion

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Geotechnical engineer report
- [ ] Soil conditions documentation
- [ ] Foundation construction records
- [ ] Weather data (drought/rain cycles)
- [ ] Seismic activity records (none expected in IL)

## 7. Neglect / Failure to Maintain

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not insure for loss caused by neglect of the insured to use all reasonable means to save and preserve property at and after a loss."

### Carrier Argument
Insured failed to take action after discovering damage, causing it to worsen.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Duties After Loss Were Complied With**
- 215 ILCS 5/154.6: Insured has 15 days to report
- Document: when was damage discovered, when was carrier notified
- Reasonable timeline = not neglect

**B. Hidden Damage**
- Damage was not visible or discoverable
- Insured could not have known to take action
- Illinois: insured not expected to have x-ray vision

**C. Carrier Delay Prevented Mitigation**
- Carrier took too long to approve mitigation
- 215 ILCS 5/154.6: 45-day decision requirement
- Additional damage from carrier delay = carrier's responsibility

**D. Mitigation Was Performed**
- Tarps placed, water extracted, temporary repairs made
- Document all mitigation efforts with photos and receipts

### Illinois Case Law
- **Cincinnati Insurance Co. v. H.D. Smith, 829 F.3d 771 (7th Cir. 2016):** Neglect exclusion does not apply when insured acted reasonably

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Timeline: discovery → notification → mitigation
- [ ] Photos: mitigation efforts (tarps, boards, etc.)
- [ ] Receipts: emergency repairs, water extraction
- [ ] Correspondence: when carrier was notified
- [ ] Expert statement: damage was not discoverable earlier

## 8. Vacancy Exclusions

### Typical Policy Language
> "If the dwelling has been vacant for more than 60 consecutive days immediately before the loss, we do not provide coverage for: vandalism, theft, water damage, or glass breakage."

### Carrier Argument
Property was vacant >60 days at time of loss → certain coverages excluded.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. Not Vacant — Just Unoccupied**
- Vacant = no personal property, no occupants
- Unoccupied = furnished but temporarily no one living there
- Document: furniture present, utilities on, mail being collected

**B. Under 60 Days**
- Count exactly: loss date minus last occupancy date
- Document: date last occupied, reason for absence

**C. Renovation in Progress**
- Property under renovation is not "vacant"
- Contractors present, work ongoing
- Document: permits, contractor schedules

**D. Seasonal Residence**
- Illinois seasonal homes (lake houses) not "vacant" during off-season
- Intended for periodic occupancy
- Document: ownership history, typical occupancy pattern

### Illinois Case Law
- **Illinois Farmers Insurance Co. v. Jones, 2016 IL App (3d) 150245:** Vacancy exclusion strictly construed; temporary absence is not vacancy

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Utility bills showing usage
- [ ] Mail forwarding records
- [ ] Photos of interior showing furnishings
- [ ] Neighbor statements confirming occupancy
- [ ] Contractor schedules (if renovation)

## 9. Cosmetic Damage Exclusions

### Typical Policy Language
> "We do not cover cosmetic damage to exterior surfaces that does not affect the functionality or weather resistance of the material."

### Carrier Argument
The damage is only cosmetic — dents, scratches, discoloration that doesn't affect performance.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. PA 103-0510 — Matching Requirements**
- Illinois law requires reasonable uniform appearance
- If matching materials unavailable → full replacement
- Cosmetic damage that cannot be matched = functional damage under IL law

**B. Granule Loss on Shingles**
- Hail causes granule loss → reduces shingle lifespan
- Not merely cosmetic — affects weather resistance
- Document: granule loss percentage, remaining shingle life

**C. Siding Dents Compromise Weather Seal**
- Dented siding may allow water infiltration
- Affects weather resistance = not merely cosmetic
- Document: potential for water infiltration

**D. Diminished Value**
- Even if "cosmetic," damage reduces property value
- Illinois recognizes diminished value claims
- Document: appraisal showing pre-loss vs. post-loss value

### Illinois Case Law
- **PA 103-0510 (enacted 2024):** Requires reasonable matching; if matching unavailable, full replacement

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Photos showing extent of "cosmetic" damage
- [ ] Contractor statement: damage affects weather resistance
- [ ] PA 103-0510 compliance analysis
- [ ] Matching material availability research
- [ ] Appraisal showing diminished value

## 10. Matching Exclusions

### Typical Policy Language
> "We will repair or replace damaged property with material of like kind and quality. We are not obligated to match undamaged materials."

### Carrier Argument
Only need to replace damaged section, not undamaged areas for matching.

### Counter-Arguments

**A. PA 103-0510 — Illinois Matching Law**
- Requires "reasonable uniform appearance"
- If matching materials unavailable → full replacement required
- Applies to roofing, siding, windows, doors
- Carriers MUST comply regardless of policy language

**B. Discontinued Materials**
- Original material no longer manufactured
- Cannot achieve "like kind and quality" without full replacement
- Document: manufacturer confirmation of discontinuation

**C. Aesthetic Continuity = Functional**
- Property function includes aesthetic value
- Mismatched siding/roofing affects property value and function
- Illinois: property damage includes loss of aesthetic value

**D. Line of Sight Argument**
- If damaged and undamaged areas are visible together
- Mismatch is apparent = damage to undamaged area's appearance
- Document: sight lines, photos showing mismatch

### Illinois Case Law
- **PA 103-0510:** Codified matching requirements
- **American Family Mutual Insurance Co. v. Krop, 2019 IL App (1st) 180893:** Pre-PA 103-0510 case supporting matching when materials unavailable

### Evidence to Gather
- [ ] Photos showing line of sight between damaged/undamaged
- [ ] Material availability confirmation from manufacturer
- [ ] Color sample comparison
- [ ] PA 103-0510 analysis
- [ ] Contractor statement: matching not possible

## Exclusion Counter-Argument Strategies

### General Strategy Framework

**1. Read the Exclusion Language Exactly**
- Do not paraphrase — read every word
- Look for: scope, exceptions, limitations on the exclusion
- Many exclusions have built-in exceptions

**2. Apply Contra Proferentem**
- Illinois: ambiguities construed against drafter (carrier)
- If exclusion language has two reasonable interpretations → favor insured
- Document the ambiguity

**3. Identify the Efficient Proximate Cause**
- Illinois: when covered and excluded perils combine
- The EFFICIENT cause determines coverage
- If covered peril set chain in motion → coverage applies

**4. Distinguish Cause from Result**
- Exclusion may cover the cause but NOT resulting damage
- Example: Excluded peril = pipe corrosion; Covered result = water damage to walls
- Resulting damage is covered

**5. Document Everything in Writing**
- Every conversation with carrier
- Every piece of evidence
- Every legal argument
- 215 ILCS 5/154.6 requires written communication

### Illinois Legal Framework for Exclusions

| Principle | Application | Citation |
|-----------|------------|----------|
| Contra Proferentem | Ambiguous exclusion language construed against carrier | 215 ILCS 5/154.6; case law |
| Reasonable Expectations | Coverage based on insured's reasonable expectations | Eddy v. Colonial Life, 59 Ill. App. 3d 645 |
| Efficient Proximate Cause | Covered peril that sets chain in motion = coverage | Country Mutual v. Harvest Bible Chapel |
| Concurrent Causation | Multiple causes, one covered = coverage | Farmers v. Martin |
| Strict Construction | Exclusions narrowly construed | Pekin v. Ward |
