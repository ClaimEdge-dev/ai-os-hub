# Exclusion Database

## Exclusion Challenge Framework

### Challenge Types
1. **Statutory Override** — Illinois statute explicitly invalidates exclusion
2. **Ambiguity** — Exclusion language is ambiguous; construed against drafter
3. **Public Policy** — Exclusion violates public policy
4. **Unconscionability** — Exclusion is unconscionable or deceptive
5. **Narrow Construction** — Exclusion must be narrowly construed

### Illinois Statutory Arsenal
- 215 ILCS 5/143: Standard fire policy provisions
- 215 ILCS 5/154: Unfair discrimination prohibited
- 215 ILCS 5/154.6: Prompt payment of claims; replacement cost
- 215 ILCS 5/155: Bad faith penalties (vexatious delay)
- 50 Ill. Adm. Code Part 919: Unfair claims practices
- 815 ILCS 505/1: Consumer Fraud Act

## Exclusion 1: Ordinance or Law
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused directly or indirectly by enforcement of any ordinance or law regulating construction, repair, or demolition."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Statutory Override:** 215 ILCS 5/154.6 requires replacement cost coverage
- **Public Policy:** Code compliance is not "betterment" but safety requirement
- **Argument:** Code upgrades are part of repair, not separate loss
**Sample Language:**
> "The Ordinance or Law exclusion cannot be applied to code-compliant repairs required by the International Residential Code. Replacement cost coverage under 215 ILCS 5/154.6 includes code-compliant materials and methods."
**Case Law:**
- *Hamel v. American Family* — Code compliance is part of repair obligation
- *Paradise v. Nowatzke* — Replacement cost includes code upgrades

## Exclusion 2: Matching / Uniform Appearance
**Common Language:** "We will not pay to repair or replace undamaged property solely for matching purposes."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Ambiguity:** "Matching" is ambiguous as applied
- **Narrow Construction:** Exclusion applies only to "solely" cosmetic matching
- **Argument:** Functional matching (waterproofing, structural) is not cosmetic
**Sample Language:**
> "The matching exclusion applies only to cosmetic appearance. When non-matching materials compromise the building envelope's integrity (water intrusion, structural continuity), replacement is not 'solely for matching' but for functional necessity."

## Exclusion 3: Cosmetic Damage
**Common Language:** "We do not cover cosmetic damage that does not affect the functionality of the property."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Ambiguity:** "Cosmetic" vs. "functional" is fact-specific
- **Argument:** Hail damage that compromises protective coating is functional
**Sample Language:**
> "Hail damage to roof shingles that removes protective granules is not 'cosmetic.' The granule layer provides UV protection and waterproofing. Loss of granules constitutes functional damage per Haag Engineering standards."

## Exclusion 4: Wear and Tear / Gradual Deterioration
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by wear and tear, deterioration, or lack of maintenance."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Causation:** Pre-existing wear does not negate covered peril damage
- **Argument:** Storm damage is distinct from pre-existing condition
**Sample Language:**
> "While the roof may have exhibited normal wear, the hail/wind event caused distinct, new damage. The proper analysis is: (1) pre-loss condition, (2) post-loss condition, (3) difference attributable to the covered peril. Wear and tear does not excuse payment for new damage."

## Exclusion 5: Faulty/Inadequate Maintenance
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by your failure to maintain the property."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Causation:** Maintenance failure must be the proximate cause
- **Argument:** Even with perfect maintenance, covered peril would have caused damage
**Sample Language:**
> "The maintenance exclusion requires the insurer to prove that proper maintenance would have prevented the loss. A well-maintained roof sustains hail damage in the same storm. The covered peril, not maintenance, is the proximate cause."

## Exclusion 6: Earth Movement
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by earth movement including sinking, rising, shifting."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Narrow Construction:** Applies only to geologic earth movement
- **Argument:** Foundation settling from water damage is not "earth movement"
**Sample Language:**
> "Foundation damage caused by water saturation from a covered plumbing leak is not 'earth movement.' The exclusion applies to geologic events (earthquakes, landslides), not hydrostatic pressure from covered water damage."

## Exclusion 7: Water Damage (Surface Water/Flood)
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss from flood, surface water, or water below ground."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Causation:** Storm-created opening allows water entry = covered
- **Argument:** Water entering through hail-damaged roof is not "flood"
**Sample Language:**
> "Water entering through roof damage caused by hail is not 'flood' damage. It is water damage from a covered peril. The hail created the opening; the water is the resulting damage, which is covered under Coverage A."

## Exclusion 8: Mold
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by mold, fungus, or wet rot."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Resulting Damage:** Mold from covered water damage is covered
- **Statutory:** 215 ILCS 5/154.6 requires prompt payment to prevent secondary damage
**Sample Language:**
> "Mold resulting from water damage caused by a covered peril is covered as resulting damage. The carrier's delay in approving the claim exacerbated the mold growth. Under 215 ILCS 5/155, the carrier is responsible for damages caused by vexatious delay."

## Exclusion 9: Neglect
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by neglect or failure to use reasonable means to save property."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Timing:** Neglect must occur before the loss
- **Argument:** Post-loss actions are mitigation, not neglect
**Sample Language:**
> "The neglect exclusion applies to pre-loss conduct, not post-loss decisions. After the storm, the homeowner's failure to immediately tarp (due to safety concerns, lack of access, or waiting for adjuster) is not 'neglect' but reasonable caution."

## Exclusion 10: Intentional Acts
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused intentionally by an insured."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Standard:** Clear and convincing evidence of intent required
- **Argument:** Damage from covered peril is never intentional
**Sample Language:**
> "The intentional acts exclusion requires clear and convincing evidence of intent to cause damage. Storm damage is inherently accidental. No reasonable interpretation supports application of this exclusion to weather-related damage."

## Exclusion 11: Business Pursuits
**Common Language:** "We do not cover liability arising from business pursuits of an insured."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Scope:** Applies only to liability (Coverage E), not property
- **Argument:** Property damage to home-based business equipment is covered under Coverage C
**Sample Language:**
> "The business pursuits exclusion applies to liability coverage only. Damage to business personal property in the home is covered under Coverage C (Personal Property) unless specifically excluded by endorsement."

## Exclusion 12: Animals
**Common Language:** "We do not cover damage caused by birds, rodents, insects, or domestic animals."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Causation:** Animal activity must be the proximate cause
- **Argument:** Entry point must be created by covered peril
**Sample Language:**
> "Squirrel entry through hail-damaged soffits is not 'animal damage.' The covered peril (hail) created the entry point. The animal damage is resulting damage from the covered peril, which is covered."

## Exclusion 13: Collapse
**Common Language:** "We do not cover collapse except as specifically provided."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Hidden Collapse Endorsement:** Many policies have hidden collapse provisions
- **Argument:** Hidden decay or insect damage may trigger collapse coverage
**Sample Language:**
> "The policy's collapse provision covers hidden decay that causes sudden collapse. The carrier must investigate whether hidden water damage (from covered peril) caused structural degradation triggering the collapse."

## Exclusion 14: Power Interruption
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss from interruption of power or other utility service."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Scope:** Applies only to off-premises power interruption
- **Argument:** On-premises electrical damage from surge is covered
**Sample Language:**
> "The power interruption exclusion applies only to off-premises utility failures. Lightning-induced power surge causing on-premises electrical damage is covered under the lightning peril."

## Exclusion 15: War / Nuclear Hazard
**ClaimEdge Note:** Never applicable to property damage claims. Listed for completeness.

## Exclusion 16: Government Action
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by seizure or destruction by governmental authority."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Scope:** Applies to confiscation, not building code enforcement
- **Argument:** Code compliance requirements are not "government action"
**Sample Language:**
> "Building code compliance requirements are regulatory, not confiscatory. The government action exclusion applies to seizure of property, not to safety standards that govern repair methods."

## Exclusion 17: Volcanic Eruption
**ClaimEdge Note:** Not applicable in Illinois. Listed for completeness.

## Exclusion 18: Mechanical Breakdown
**Common Language:** "We do not cover mechanical or electrical breakdown."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Causation:** Breakdown caused by covered peril is covered
- **Argument:** Hail damage to HVAC condenser is not "breakdown"
**Sample Language:**
> "Hail damage to the HVAC condenser coils is not 'mechanical breakdown.' It is physical damage from a covered peril. The equipment was functioning properly before the storm."

## Exclusion 19: Smog / Corrosion
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by corrosion, smog, or industrial smoke."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Causation:** Must be the proximate cause
- **Argument:** Storm damage creates entry point for corrosion
**Sample Language:**
> "The corrosion exclusion applies to gradual chemical deterioration, not to rust that forms after storm damage compromises protective coatings. The covered peril initiated the conditions that led to corrosion."

## Exclusion 20: Settling / Shrinking
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by settling, shrinking, or expansion."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Scope:** Applies to normal construction settling
- **Argument:** Storm-caused structural movement is not settling
**Sample Language:**
> "Foundation movement caused by hydrostatic pressure from storm water is not 'settling.' It is damage from a covered water event. The exclusion applies to normal construction settlement over time."

## Exclusion 21: Weight of Ice/Snow/Sleet
**Common Language:** "We do not cover damage from weight of ice, snow, or sleet unless policy specifically provides."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Check Policy:** Some Illinois policies include this peril
- **Argument:** Collapse from weight may trigger collapse coverage
**Sample Language:**
> "If the policy excludes weight of snow but includes collapse, the snow-induced roof collapse is covered under the collapse provision. The carrier must investigate the structural failure, not just the load cause."

## Exclusion 22: Dampness/Dry Rot
**Common Language:** "We do not cover loss caused by dampness or dry rot."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Causation:** Dampness from covered water damage is covered
- **Argument:** Dry rot is resulting damage, not excluded cause
**Sample Language:**
> "Dry rot that develops after a covered plumbing leak is resulting damage. The covered peril (pipe burst) created the moisture condition. The carrier is responsible for all resulting damage including dry rot remediation."

## Exclusion 23: Rust / Oxidation
**Similar to Exclusion 19. Challenge using causation arguments.**

## Exclusion 24: Smoke from Agricultural Operations
**ClaimEdge Note:** Rarely applicable in residential claims. Challenge if misapplied to wildfire smoke damage.**

## Exclusion 25: Discharge/Dispersal of Pollutants
**Common Language:** "We do not cover discharge, dispersal, seepage, or release of pollutants."
**Challenge Strategy:**
- **Scope:** Applies to industrial pollution, not household materials
- **Argument:** Asbestos from disturbed building materials is not "pollution"
**Sample Language:**
> "Asbestos fibers released during storm damage to siding/roofing are not 'pollutants' under the exclusion. The exclusion targets industrial chemical releases, not naturally occurring building materials disturbed by covered perils."

## Combination Strategies

### Strategy A: Exclusion Stacking
When carrier applies multiple exclusions, argue that:
1. Each exclusion must independently exclude the loss
2. Exclusions cannot be combined to create coverage gap
3. Ambiguity in any one exclusion requires coverage

### Strategy B: Resulting Damage Doctrine
Even if cause is excluded, argue that:
1. Resulting damage from covered peril is covered
2. Secondary damage is not excluded unless explicitly stated
3. Carrier must pay for repair of resulting damage

### Strategy C: Concurrent Causation
When covered and excluded perils both contribute:
1. Illinois follows efficient proximate cause doctrine
2. If covered peril is the efficient cause, full coverage applies
3. Carrier cannot apportion when causes are inseparable
